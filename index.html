Cevian Downside Test

Max-Loss Sizing (20% Downside Rule)

Cevian's published risk framework requires that even in the worst reasonable scenario, a position should not lose more than ~20%. Applied to each idea:
Bayer — Downside
Base floor: €33/share (-20%). Assumes Roundup Supreme Court ruling goes against Bayer and 2027 pharma growth disappoints and no break-up emerges. Even then, Pharma alone (~€4.5bn EBITDA × 8× = €36bn EV) plus Consumer Health (~€1.4bn × 12× = €17bn) broadly covers the €33bn of net debt and pension. Crop Science becomes effectively free equity.

The structural protection: three different business cycles (pharma, ag, OTC), three different customer types, and three different regulatory environments. Correlated shock requires all three to miss simultaneously.
Ahold Delhaize — Downside
Base floor: €33/share (-20%). Would require US gross margin to collapse (Aldi / Walmart price war intensifies materially) and European margin pressure in CSE to compound. Even so, the group would still generate €1.5-2bn of FCF and return ~€1bn/year via buyback. Multiple floor around 6× EBITDA in that scenario.

The structural protection: grocery is the most defensive large-cap sector in Europe. Comparable drawdowns during GFC and COVID were single-digit. Food Lion's 53 consecutive quarters of comp growth is the hard data behind the soft claim.
DHL Group — Downside
Base floor: €40/share (-20%). Assumes a deeper global trade recession + Trump tariffs on parcels escalate + Verdi-driven wage settlement above plan in Post & Parcel Germany. Even in that case, the Express margin is protected by the duopoly structure (with UPS) against FedEx on transatlantic lanes, and the "Fit for Growth" €1bn cost programme releases in 2027.

The structural protection: letter mail decline is a known, priced, multi-decade structural trend. Post & Parcel Germany can raise prices by government decree when volumes fall — the regulator approved a +4.4% letter-mail price increase for 2025-27, and that mechanism continues.
Style Synthesis

How These Ideas Fit Cevian's Playbook

The style I'm trying to show

These three names are chosen specifically to blend four influences: (i) my own preference for SOTP-driven stories with verifiable segment-level EBITDA (the same analytical spine as the Amadeus pitch); (ii) Cevian's "neglected or misunderstood in out-of-favour industries" filter (Förberg / Hedge Fund Journal, 2007); (iii) Andrew's emphasis on the governance mechanism — activism only works when there is a plausible board-level intervention, not a press release; and (iv) my dad's long-held view that the best European activist opportunities come from German industrial and Dutch holding-company structures, where the conglomerate discount is both durable and, when it finally closes, violent.

All three ideas have the same structural shape: (a) a crown-jewel business that would trade at 10-15× on its own, (b) one or more encumbering legacy / declining / regulated businesses dragging the multiple down, (c) an incumbent CEO publicly resistant to breakup, (d) a credible catalyst window in 24-36 months. This is the ABB (2012-18) playbook with Power Grids; the ThyssenKrupp (2013-) playbook with the elevator spin; the Pearson playbook with the Penguin Random House exit. Cevian has run this exact pattern seven times in the last fifteen years.

§ 2.2 — What You Own

Three Businesses, Three Different Cycles

Bayer is not really one company — it is three operationally independent businesses that happen to share a balance sheet. Each has its own customer base, its own competitors, its own regulator, and its own capital cycle. The strategic logic for keeping them together is weak; the strategic logic for separating them is the entire thesis.
Pharmaceuticals
~€18bn revenue · 24-26% EBITDA margin · ~€4.5bn EBITDA

Specialty-heavy prescription pharma. The 2026 picture is defined by Xarelto patent cliff (the anticoagulant, Bayer's largest drug, off-patent outside the US by end 2026 — the company has publicly framed 2026 as the "final year" of the cliff) and two accelerating new products — Nubeqa (prostate cancer, +77% in H1 2025 currency-adjusted) and Kerendia (diabetic kidney disease, +86%). Beyond 2027 the pipeline includes elinzanetant (menopause vasomotor symptoms, FDA decision pending) and the Beyonttra launch.

Comparable: Sanofi trades at ~8× EV/EBITDA; Merck KGaA at ~12×; GSK at ~8-9×. A standalone Bayer Pharma, post-cliff and with a launched pipeline, should trade at 9-10×.
Crop Science
~€23bn revenue · 18-20% EBITDA margin · ~€4.4bn EBITDA

Inherited from the 2018 Monsanto acquisition ($63bn). #1 or #2 globally in seeds and traits, #1 in herbicides. Corn seed + traits grew +7% in Q3 2025. Seasonal, commodity-exposed via glyphosate prices, and still carries the Roundup litigation tail — but the price in that tail is recovering toward historical medians (per Bayer's own Q3 2025 commentary) as Chinese oversupply normalises.

Comparable: Corteva announced in early 2026 that it will separate Seed from Crop Protection by Q4 2026Source: Corteva CEO Chuck Magro at Bank of America Global Agriculture Conference; TIKR reporting Feb 2026. Operating EBITDA guidance 2026: $4.0-4.2bn. Announced separation into two listed companies Q4 2026. — EV/EBITDA around 14×. FMC at ~9×. Blended Crop Science multiple: 10-12× is defensible for Bayer's broader portfolio.
Consumer Health
~€5.8bn revenue · 23-24% EBITDA margin · ~€1.4bn EBITDA

The most separable and most acquirable of the three. Global OTC portfolio: Aspirin, Claritin, Berocca, Elevit, Bepanthen, Alka-Seltzer, Canesten. Category-leading brands in Dermatology, Digestive Health, Pain & Cardio. Q3 2025 growth in Derma, Digestive and Pain/Cardio offsetting softness in Allergy & Cold. Margin stable at 23-24%.

Comparable: Haleon (LSE) at 14.9× EV/Adj. EBITDA; Kenvue at 14.1× — and Kenvue is being acquired by Kimberly-Clark (close H2 2026). Opella (ex-Sanofi CHC) sold 50% to CD&R at ~14×. The Consumer Health multiple for a pure-play is firmly in the 13-15× zone.

The strategic contradiction

There is no operational logic connecting these three businesses. A corn farmer in Iowa, a cardiologist in Berlin, and a mother buying children's Claritin in Sydney share no procurement, no regulator, no technology stack. Combining them doesn't create scale economies — it creates coordination diseconomies. Bayer under the prior CEO had 12 layers of management; Anderson has compressed that significantly via Dynamic Shared Ownership, but the fundamental point stands: this is a holding company pretending to be an operating company.

§ 2.5 — The Activist Unlock

What Cevian Can Actually Do

The Mechanism
Bayer is a Supervisory Board (Aufsichtsrat) company under German co-determination. Half the board is employee representatives; half is shareholder representatives. Cevian's historical playbook in Germany (ThyssenKrupp, Bilfinger) has been to engage on the shareholder side of the supervisory board — putting people around the table in nomination and strategy committees, not in the press.

The specific ask is not an immediate full three-way break-up. It is a credible, board-endorsed, publicly-timelined Consumer Health separation — the path of least resistance, least tax friction, and most obvious comparable (Haleon / Kenvue / Opella). Once that is announced and executed, Crop Science separation becomes a 2028-29 conversation from a position of strength.
Why Consumer Health first
Four reasons. (1) Cleanest separation — no shared IP with Pharma, no shared manufacturing with Crop. (2) Clearest comp — Haleon / Kenvue / Opella set the multiple. (3) Anderson's stated objection (2024 earnings call: Consumer Health is "reliably generating cash flow") is exactly why it is most valuable to someone else — a pure-play CHC operator would pay up for predictable category cash flow. (4) Proceeds reduce Bayer net debt — an €18bn CHC sale at 13× EBITDA would close the deleveraging gap and remove the strategic argument against further separation.

Bluebell's 2023 letter and Inclusive Capital's subsequent position make this the publicly-understood activist ask. Cevian entering would not be a surprise attack — it would be a collaborative acceleration of a strategic review that is already in the board's inbox.
§ 2.6 — Key Risks & Mitigants
Risk — Supreme Court loses
If the Supreme Court rules against Bayer on Roundup, the litigation tail re-opens. Mitigant: Bayer has already provisioned $10bn+ and is currently structured to survive a loss — the $7.25bn settlement covers ~90% of known cases. A loss would hit sentiment but not solvency. Anderson's Axios interview (Apr 2026): "experts believe the company will win."
Risk — CEO resistance hardens
Anderson continues to publicly defend the three-in-one structure (March 2026: "Amazon operates successfully across several distinct industries"). Mitigant: Supervisory Board composition is the mechanism. Co-determination slows change but does not prevent it — ThyssenKrupp proves this. Cevian's collaborative approach is specifically designed for this governance structure.
Risk — Pharma pipeline disappoints
If Nubeqa / Kerendia / elinzanetant miss expectations, 2027 growth inflection gets pushed. Mitigant: Nubeqa and Kerendia are already launched and posting 70%+ growth. The pipeline risk is distribution, not discovery — and the Consumer Health / Crop Science separation case does not depend on Pharma growth.
Risk — Already rallied hard
The biggest risk to the entry price. Stock is +94% off €21 lows. Much of the Roundup-clearing trade is now in the price. Mitigant: Pre-rally Bayer traded on "litigation discount + broken conglomerate." Post-rally it trades on "broken conglomerate only." The remaining gap is ~€15-30bn of equity — all of which closes through a break-up, not a re-rating. That's still a 5:1 risk/reward at current entry, which is within Cevian's published frame.
§ 2.7 — Why Cevian Specifically
Bayer is the kind of idea that only a handful of activists on earth can run: (a) cheque size — taking a meaningful stake in a €40bn company requires €2-4bn of capital allocation, above the reach of Bluebell or Petrus; (b) time horizon — a three-way break-up executes over 3-5 years, beyond the typical US activist patience; (c) governance familiarity — co-determination and the Aufsichtsrat system is where Cevian has spent the last 15 years (ThyssenKrupp, Bilfinger, RWE-era engagements); (d) credibility — Bluebell and Inclusive Capital have already done the press-release work; the board now needs a collaborative counterparty to execute, not another one to lecture. That's Cevian's sweet spot, and the reason the name sits at the top of this list despite the recent rally.

§ 3.2 — What You Actually Own

A US Company and a European Company Stapled Together

The group reports two operating segments: "The United States" and "Europe". In reality, these are entirely separate businesses — different customers, different competitors, different supply chains, different regulators. They share a headquarters in Zaandam and very little else.
The US Business
~€52bn revenue · 4.7% underlying op. margin · ~€2.4bn op. income · ~€4.0bn EBITDA

Five East-Coast banners: Food Lion (south-east, 1,100+ stores, 53-quarter comp streak), Stop & Shop (Northeast, 350 stores, in active turnaround), Hannaford (New England, 180 stores), Giant Food (DC / Virginia, 160 stores), and The GIANT Company (Pennsylvania, 190 stores). Online sales +22.8% at constant currency in Q4 2025.

Strategic point: this is not a troubled US business in a European conglomerate. It is a stable, growing US grocery business — Food Lion in particular is one of the best compounders in US grocery — that happens to be wrapped in a Dutch listing.
The Europe Business
~€40bn revenue · ~3.7% underlying op. margin · ~€1.5bn op. income · ~€2.8bn EBITDA (incl. bol)

Three distinct sub-regions: Benelux — Albert Heijn (Netherlands, record 38.2% national market shareSource: Ahold Delhaize Q4 2025 results (Feb 2026). The geographic mismatch

60% of revenue is US. 60%+ of group EBITDA is US. The US business grew 4.7% operating margin in 2025 with online sales up 22.8% and pharmacy up double-digits. Yet the group listing is in Amsterdam, the index is AEX, and the primary broker coverage is European (ING, Kepler, Citi). American investors — including Kroger's own investor base, Walmart's investor base, Costco's investor base — are simply not natural owners of AD:NA. This is the structural cause of the valuation discount, not the consequence.

Why the unlock is bigger than the SOTP alone

The SOTP number is conservative on purpose. The real unlock — the thing that makes this a 5:1 Cevian idea rather than a 2:1 one — is capital-return acceleration post-separation. A US-listed AD US would do two things simultaneously: (a) access US institutional capital directly, closing the passive-ownership discount; (b) free up the parent balance sheet to return another €2-3bn/year via buyback at a higher multiple, compounding much faster than the current €1bn programme. The combined effect over a 3-year hold is 35-50% total return versus a 15-25% total return if nothing changes. This is the classic Cevian compounding pattern: fair-valued downside, 5:1 upside through a structural change that is the incumbent board's to own.

§ 3.5 — The Activist Unlock

What Cevian Can Actually Do

The Ask
Primary: dual-listing of the US business on NYSE (parent retains 70-80%) to close the passive-ownership discount. Secondary: carve-out IPO of bol.com (parent retains 80%) to recognise its standalone digital-commerce valuation. Tertiary: operating-margin catch-up to Kroger/peer levels in the US through continued price investment + Prism monetisation.

The ask is not break-up — Ahold Delhaize has better cross-regional learning than most retail conglomerates (Save for Our Customers programme delivered €1.35bn of savings in 2025). It is listing optimisation. This is a lower bar than a split and the board can approve it without operational disruption.
The Governance
Ahold Delhaize is a Dutch NV with a two-tier board (Management Board + Supervisory Board). Frans Muller (CEO since 2018) is widely expected to retire in the next 2-3 years — creating a natural transition window for strategic review. There is no incumbent activist on the register. Cevian's 2025 arrival at Akzo Nobel (also Dutch) demonstrates the firm is now operating comfortably in the Dutch governance environment.

Entry point: a 2-3% stake (~€700m-1bn, well within Cevian's typical check size) is enough to secure supervisory board consultation. 5% is the threshold at which Dutch disclosure kicks in — Cevian's preferred size.
§ 3.6 — Key Risks & Mitigants
Risk — US price war accelerates
Walmart, Costco, and Aldi are all pushing price aggressively. Aldi announced a large US expansion for 2026. Mitigant: Food Lion's 53-quarter comp streak includes the 2023-25 period of maximum inflation-driven price competition. Ahold's US banners are regional leaders, not national scale-players — they compete on local assortment and service, not on the national price spear tip. The price-war risk is structurally lower for Food Lion than for Kroger or Albertsons.
Risk — IRA pharmacy drag
The 2026 guidance calls out a ~$350m negative impact on US reported sales from the Inflation Reduction Act. Mitigant: Ahold has explicitly stated there is no impact to underlying operating income — pharmacy is low-margin revenue and the hit is a reclassification, not a cash-flow loss. The market needs a quarter or two to digest this distinction; Cevian can use that volatility to add.
Risk — Board resistance to US listing
Dutch NV boards have historically resisted dilution of home-market control. Mitigant: a dual listing is not a relisting — home market remains Amsterdam; parent retains control. Multiple precedents exist: Shell's NL/UK dual structure (collapsed 2022), Unilever's historical dual structure. The ask is listing access, not listing relocation.
Risk — Serbia / CSE politicisation
Q4 2025 European margin was hit by the Serbian government's price-cap intervention on grocery. Mitigant: CSE is ~5% of group revenue, not margin-critical. The 2023 Profi acquisition is still integrating and margin should normalise. This is the smallest risk on the page.

§ 4.2 — What You Actually Own

Five Businesses — One Crown Jewel, One Run-off, Three in the Middle

The group reports five divisions. Their strategic situations are radically different: Express is a defended global duopoly (DHL-UPS outside the US; FedEx the third globally). Post & Parcel Germany is regulated letter mail in structural run-off. The other three are stable but cyclical.

The conglomerate logic — or lack of it

DHL Express and Post & Parcel Germany share a brand, a Bonn headquarters, a historical origin in Deutsche Bundespost — and almost nothing else operationally. Express uses a global air network; PPG uses a German truck and walk-in fleet. Express sells to multinational corporates; PPG sells to German consumers and small businesses. Express earns 11-12% EBIT margins on global trade flows; PPG earns 6% on regulated domestic mail. Keeping them together subsidises one with the credibility of the other, but the market prices the combined entity at the weaker multiple. This is exactly the structural pattern Cevian broke at ABB (Power Grids carve-out) and at ThyssenKrupp (elevator spin-off).

§ 4.3 — Why Express Deserves the FedEx/UPS Multiple

The Express Franchise

Structural Position
Three integrators globally. That's it. FedEx, UPS, DHL Express. No fourth. Amazon has logistics capacity but does not offer time-definite international express at scale. Time-definite international (TDI) is the specific product category where customers cannot substitute — if your cargo is (a) medical/diagnostic samples, (b) regulated-pharmaceutical cold chain, (c) automotive or semiconductor parts where production-line downtime cost exceeds shipping cost, you use one of these three. Routing choice is frequently set at a customer's global procurement level and is sticky.

DHL's geographic bias is Europe-Asia and intra-Europe, where UPS is thinner. On those lanes DHL Express is effectively a duopoly with FedEx. The 11.8% EBIT margin in Q3 2025 — with trade volumes falling — demonstrates the pricing power.
Why It's Not Priced That Way
Four reasons the group multiple compresses the Express multiple:

(1) Post & Parcel Germany's secular letter-mail decline drags the blended growth profile. (2) State-linked ownership — KfW holds ~16% and the German government has implicit influence over strategic decisions via the regulated postal business. (3) Verdi labour relations in Germany — wage settlements are a recurring overhang. (4) Global Forwarding volatility — this segment's earnings swing with freight rates and creates noise around cleaner Express numbers.

A standalone DHL Express would eliminate all four overhangs in one step. FedEx's $85bn EV at 10-11× EBITDA on broadly similar EBITDA ($8bn) is the reference point. UPS is similar. Standalone DHL Express at 10-11× → €45-50bn EV, captured inside a group EV of €79bn today.

§ 4.5 — The Activist Unlock

What Cevian Can Actually Do

The Ask
Primary: legal separation of Post & Parcel Germany into a standalone, German-listed regulated postal utility (parent retains 50-75% initially, reduces over time). Immediate effect: group re-prices on the remaining Express / Supply Chain / Global Forwarding / eCommerce mix — a cleaner, higher-margin, internationally-comparable logistics franchise that trades at a blended 9-10× rather than a blended 7× today.

Secondary: accelerate the "Fit for Growth" €1bn cost programme into 2026 rather than full impact in 2027. Tertiary: increase the €6bn buyback cadence as FCF strengthens post-separation.

This is the ABB Power Grids carve-out template, run in the opposite direction: at ABB, Cevian pushed to sell a legacy-heavy unit (Power Grids to Hitachi) while keeping the growth core. At DHL, the ask is to list the legacy-heavy unit (PPG) while keeping the growth core. Same thesis, different structural lever.
The Governance Complexity
This is the hardest of the three names to execute on. Three complicating factors:

(1) KfW (German state development bank) holds ~16% — per Public Technologies April 2026 reporting, BlackRock raised its voting-rights stake to 6.6% around the same time, making KfW and BlackRock the two largest shareholders. KfW is a political actor as much as a financial one.

(2) Verdi (the German services union) is organised across Post & Parcel Germany and has mobilised against restructuring before. The 2025 announcement of 8,000 headcount reductions was accompanied by significant labour protest. Any separation triggers labour co-determination process under German Mitbestimmung.

(3) Regulated-postal licence obligations — under German postal law, the "universal service" obligation (daily mail delivery to every German household) cannot simply be shed. A PPG separation must carry the regulatory framework with it, which is a multi-year legislative process.

None of this is fatal — Cevian's precedent at ThyssenKrupp (IG Metall, state-linked shareholders, German co-determination) is directly analogous. But it makes this a 3-5 year engagement, not a 2-year one.
§ 4.6 — Key Risks & Mitigants
Risk — Trade war intensifies
Trump-era tariffs + elimination of the US de minimis exemption for low-value shipments (effective August 2025) hit Express volumes to the US. DHL US CEO called out the volume impact explicitly. Mitigant: Express delivered +7.6% group EBIT growth in Q3 2025 despite the tariff impact, through active capacity management and pricing. The tariff volatility is asymmetric — down-volume, up-price — and the net effect on EBIT margin has been positive (7.3% in Q3 2025 vs 6.7% prior year).
Risk — KfW blocks separation
As a state-linked 16% shareholder, KfW could credibly oppose a PPG separation. Mitigant: KfW's mandate is to support German economic development, and a cleanly-priced PPG (trading as a regulated utility with a ~5% dividend yield) is arguably more aligned with that mandate than a sprawling conglomerate. Cevian's relationship-building playbook is designed for exactly this situation — this is where their five-year horizon and collaborative posture matter most.
Risk — Verdi wage settlement
German labour contract at PPG expires and any wage settlement above productivity flows straight to margin. Mitigant: PPG margins can be repaired by letter-mail price increases (regulated, recently approved at +4.4% for 2025-27 cycle) and by parcel business growth. Structurally the labour risk is confined to PPG, which is exactly the unit the thesis seeks to separate.
Risk — Global Forwarding cycle extends
Freight rates have been weak for 18+ months. Q3 2025 Global Forwarding revenue down 9.2%. Mitigant: Forwarding is ~8% of EBIT; even a -30% from here would reduce group EBIT by ~4-5%. It is not the thesis-critical segment. The crown jewel (Express) and the cash-cow (Supply Chain) are both growing profits in the current environment.

§ 5.4 — Portfolio Fit

Three shapes, one playbook

Each of Bayer, Ahold Delhaize and DHL Group represents a different degree of the same activist pattern: a crown-jewel business held down by a structural encumbrance. Bayer is the most extreme version — three separately valuable units, with the CEO publicly resistant to change and the litigation overhang providing political cover. Ahold Delhaize is the simplest — a listing-venue arbitrage, no break-up required, friendly board environment. DHL is the most structurally analogous to ABB — a genuinely world-class franchise (Express) trapped inside a regulated German legacy (Post & Parcel), with a five-year political process required to separate them.

If I could only pitch one in a coffee chat, I would pitch Ahold Delhaize — it is the most actionable, has the fewest overhangs, and fits the Dutch channel Cevian has just opened with Akzo Nobel. If the chat extended into a structural discussion of activist mechanism, I would pivot to DHL Group — it is the best illustration of how Cevian's collaborative style works where Elliott's would not. I would mention Bayer as a counter-point — the idea that should be in the portfolio but isn't yet, and the post-rally entry-price honesty.
