Cevian Downside Test

Max-Loss Sizing (20% Downside Rule)

Cevian's published risk framework requires that even in the worst reasonable scenario, a position should not lose more than ~20%. Applied to each idea:
Ahold — €33 floor
Would require US gross margin to collapse + European pressure. Even so, €1.5-2bn FCF, €1bn/yr buyback, multiple floor 6× EBITDA. Grocery sector drawdowns in GFC and COVID were single-digit. Structural protection is category, not execution.
DHL — €40 floor

Bayer — €33 floor
Requires Roundup Supreme Court loss + 2027 pharma disappointment + no break-up. Even then, Pharma (€4.5bn EBITDA × 8×) + Consumer Health (€1.4bn × 12×) covers net debt; Crop Science becomes free option.
Volkswagen — €75 floor
This is where VW fails the test. Porsche AG already at €43 (close to all-time low); China share loss has permanent and cumulative features; EV capex does not ease. A -30% scenario is not extraordinary — it's a mid-case if two of three known headwinds compound. 20% downside discipline cannot be maintained.
Style Synthesis

How These Ideas Fit Cevian's Playbook

The style I'm trying to show

The four names blend four influences: (i) my own preference for SOTP-driven stories with verifiable segment-level EBITDA (same analytical spine as the Amadeus pitch); (ii) Cevian's "neglected or misunderstood in out-of-favour industries" filter (Förberg / Hedge Fund Journal, 2007); (iii) Andrew's emphasis on the governance mechanism — activism only works when there is a plausible board-level intervention, not a press release; and (iv) my dad's long-held view that the best European activist opportunities come from German industrial and Dutch holding-company structures, where the conglomerate discount is both durable and, when it finally closes, violent.

The first three ideas share a structural shape: (a) a crown-jewel business that would trade at 10-15× on its own, (b) one or more encumbering legacy / declining / regulated businesses dragging the multiple down, (c) an incumbent CEO publicly resistant to breakup, (d) a credible catalyst window in 24-36 months. This is the ABB (2012-18) playbook with Power Grids; the ThyssenKrupp (2013-) playbook with the elevator spin; the Pearson playbook with the Penguin Random House exit. Cevian has run this exact pattern seven times in the last fifteen years.

Volkswagen is included deliberately as a counter-example. On pure SOTP math it looks like the single best idea on this page. In practice, its three-way ownership lock (Porsche SE + Lower Saxony + Qatar) means Cevian cannot buy a board seat even with a €3bn stake — the voting control simply does not move. The discipline of rejecting a visibly-cheap name because the unlock mechanism does not work is, in my view, the single most important thing to get right in a Cevian interview.

§ 2.2 — What You Own

Three Businesses, Three Different Cycles

Bayer is not really one company — it is three operationally independent businesses that happen to share a balance sheet. Each has its own customer base, its own competitors, its own regulator, and its own capital cycle. The strategic logic for keeping them together is weak; the strategic logic for separating them is the entire thesis.
Pharmaceuticals
~€18bn revenue · 24-26% EBITDA margin · ~€4.5bn EBITDA

Specialty-heavy prescription pharma. The 2026 picture is defined by Xarelto patent cliff (the anticoagulant, Bayer's largest drug, off-patent outside the US by end 2026 — the company has publicly framed 2026 as the "final year" of the cliff) and two accelerating new products — Nubeqa (prostate cancer, +77% in H1 2025 currency-adjusted) and Kerendia (diabetic kidney disease, +86%). Beyond 2027 the pipeline includes elinzanetant (menopause vasomotor symptoms, FDA decision pending) and the Beyonttra launch.

Comparable: Sanofi trades at ~8× EV/EBITDA; Merck KGaA at ~12×; GSK at ~8-9×. A standalone Bayer Pharma, post-cliff and with a launched pipeline, should trade at 9-10×.
Crop Science
~€23bn revenue · 18-20% EBITDA margin · ~€4.4bn EBITDA

Inherited from the 2018 Monsanto acquisition ($63bn). #1 or #2 globally in seeds and traits, #1 in herbicides. Corn seed + traits grew +7% in Q3 2025. Seasonal, commodity-exposed via glyphosate prices, and still carries the Roundup litigation tail — but the price in that tail is recovering toward historical medians (per Bayer's own Q3 2025 commentary) as Chinese oversupply normalises.

Comparable: Corteva announced in early 2026 that it will separate Seed from Crop Protection by Q4 2026Source: Corteva CEO Chuck Magro at Bank of America Global Agriculture Conference; TIKR reporting Feb 2026. Operating EBITDA guidance 2026: $4.0-4.2bn. Announced separation into two listed companies Q4 2026. — EV/EBITDA around 14×. FMC at ~9×. Blended Crop Science multiple: 10-12× is defensible for Bayer's broader portfolio.
Consumer Health
~€5.8bn revenue · 23-24% EBITDA margin · ~€1.4bn EBITDA

The most separable and most acquirable of the three. Global OTC portfolio: Aspirin, Claritin, Berocca, Elevit, Bepanthen, Alka-Seltzer, Canesten. Category-leading brands in Dermatology, Digestive Health, Pain & Cardio. Q3 2025 growth in Derma, Digestive and Pain/Cardio offsetting softness in Allergy & Cold. Margin stable at 23-24%.

Comparable: Haleon (LSE) at 14.9× EV/Adj. EBITDA; Kenvue at 14.1× — and Kenvue is being acquired by Kimberly-Clark (close H2 2026). Opella (ex-Sanofi CHC) sold 50% to CD&R at ~14×. The Consumer Health multiple for a pure-play is firmly in the 13-15× zone.

The strategic contradiction

There is no operational logic connecting these three businesses. A corn farmer in Iowa, a cardiologist in Berlin, and a mother buying children's Claritin in Sydney share no procurement, no regulator, no technology stack. Combining them doesn't create scale economies — it creates coordination diseconomies. Bayer under the prior CEO had 12 layers of management; Anderson has compressed that significantly via Dynamic Shared Ownership, but the fundamental point stands: this is a holding company pretending to be an operating company.

§ 2.5 — The Activist Unlock

What Cevian Can Actually Do

The Mechanism
Bayer is a Supervisory Board (Aufsichtsrat) company under German co-determination. Half the board is employee representatives; half is shareholder representatives. Cevian's historical playbook in Germany (ThyssenKrupp, Bilfinger) has been to engage on the shareholder side of the supervisory board — putting people around the table in nomination and strategy committees, not in the press.

The specific ask is not an immediate full three-way break-up. It is a credible, board-endorsed, publicly-timelined Consumer Health separation — the path of least resistance, least tax friction, and most obvious comparable (Haleon / Kenvue / Opella). Once that is announced and executed, Crop Science separation becomes a 2028-29 conversation from a position of strength.
Why Consumer Health first
Four reasons. (1) Cleanest separation — no shared IP with Pharma, no shared manufacturing with Crop. (2) Clearest comp — Haleon / Kenvue / Opella set the multiple. (3) Anderson's stated objection (2024 earnings call: Consumer Health is "reliably generating cash flow") is exactly why it is most valuable to someone else — a pure-play CHC operator would pay up for predictable category cash flow. (4) Proceeds reduce Bayer net debt — an €18bn CHC sale at 13× EBITDA would close the deleveraging gap and remove the strategic argument against further separation.

Bluebell's 2023 letter and Inclusive Capital's subsequent position make this the publicly-understood activist ask. Cevian entering would not be a surprise attack — it would be a collaborative acceleration of a strategic review that is already in the board's inbox.
§ 2.6 — Key Risks & Mitigants
Risk — Supreme Court loses
If the Supreme Court rules against Bayer on Roundup, the litigation tail re-opens. Mitigant: Bayer has already provisioned $10bn+ and is currently structured to survive a loss — the $7.25bn settlement covers ~90% of known cases. A loss would hit sentiment but not solvency. Anderson's Axios interview (Apr 2026): "experts believe the company will win."
Risk — CEO resistance hardens
Anderson continues to publicly defend the three-in-one structure (March 2026: "Amazon operates successfully across several distinct industries"). Mitigant: Supervisory Board composition is the mechanism. Co-determination slows change but does not prevent it — ThyssenKrupp proves this. Cevian's collaborative approach is specifically designed for this governance structure.
Risk — Pharma pipeline disappoints
If Nubeqa / Kerendia / elinzanetant miss expectations, 2027 growth inflection gets pushed. Mitigant: Nubeqa and Kerendia are already launched and posting 70%+ growth. The pipeline risk is distribution, not discovery — and the Consumer Health / Crop Science separation case does not depend on Pharma growth.
Risk — Already rallied hard
The biggest risk to the entry price. Stock is +94% off €21 lows. Much of the Roundup-clearing trade is now in the price. Mitigant: Pre-rally Bayer traded on "litigation discount + broken conglomerate." Post-rally it trades on "broken conglomerate only." The remaining gap is ~€15-30bn of equity — all of which closes through a break-up, not a re-rating. That's still a 5:1 risk/reward at current entry, which is within Cevian's published frame.
§ 2.7 — Why Cevian Specifically
Bayer is the kind of idea that only a handful of activists on earth can run: (a) cheque size — taking a meaningful stake in a €40bn company requires €2-4bn of capital allocation, above the reach of Bluebell or Petrus; (b) time horizon — a three-way break-up executes over 3-5 years, beyond the typical US activist patience; (c) governance familiarity — co-determination and the Aufsichtsrat system is where Cevian has spent the last 15 years (ThyssenKrupp, Bilfinger, RWE-era engagements); (d) credibility — Bluebell and Inclusive Capital have already done the press-release work; the board now needs a collaborative counterparty to execute, not another one to lecture. That's Cevian's sweet spot, and the reason the name sits at the top of this list despite the recent rally.

Why the unlock is bigger than the SOTP alone

The SOTP number is conservative on purpose. The real unlock — the thing that makes this a 5:1 Cevian idea rather than a 2:1 one — is capital-return acceleration post-separation. § 4.3 — Why Express Deserves the FedEx/UPS Multiple

The Express Franchise

Structural Position
Three integrators globally. That's it. FedEx, UPS, DHL Express. No fourth. Amazon has logistics capacity but does not offer time-definite international express at scale. Time-definite international (TDI) is the specific product category where customers cannot substitute — if your cargo is (a) medical/diagnostic samples, (b) regulated-pharmaceutical cold chain, (c) automotive or semiconductor parts where production-line downtime cost exceeds shipping cost, you use one of these three. Routing choice is frequently set at a customer's global procurement level and is sticky.

DHL's geographic bias is Europe-Asia and intra-Europe, where UPS is thinner. On those lanes DHL Express is effectively a duopoly with FedEx. The 11.8% EBIT margin in Q3 2025 — with trade volumes falling — demonstrates the pricing power.
Why It's Not Priced That Way
Four reasons the group multiple compresses the Express multiple:

(1) Post & Parcel Germany's secular letter-mail decline drags the blended growth profile. (2) State-linked ownership — KfW holds ~16% and the German government has implicit influence over strategic decisions via the regulated postal business. (3) Verdi labour relations in Germany — wage settlements are a recurring overhang. (4) Global Forwarding volatility — this segment's earnings swing with freight rates and creates noise around cleaner Express numbers.

A standalone DHL Express would eliminate all four overhangs in one step. FedEx's $85bn EV at 10-11× EBITDA on broadly similar EBITDA ($8bn) is the reference point. UPS is similar. Standalone DHL Express at 10-11× → €45-50bn EV, captured inside a group EV of €79bn today.

The Governance Complexity
This is the hardest of the three names to execute on. Three complicating factors:

(1) KfW (German state development bank) holds ~16% — per Public Technologies April 2026 reporting, BlackRock raised its voting-rights stake to 6.6% around the same time, making KfW and BlackRock the two largest shareholders. KfW is a political actor as much as a financial one.

(2) Verdi (the German services union) is organised across Post & Parcel Germany and has mobilised against restructuring before. (3) Regulated-postal licence obligations — under German postal law, the "universal service" obligation (daily mail delivery to every German household) cannot simply be shed. A PPG separation must carry the regulatory framework with it, which is a multi-year legislative process.

None of this is fatal — Cevian's precedent at ThyssenKrupp (IG Metall, state-linked shareholders, German co-determination) is directly analogous. But it makes this a 3-5 year engagement, not a 2-year one.

§ 5.3 — Why the Discount Persists

Three Structural Reasons, Each of Which Fails the Cevian Filter

Reason 1 — Ownership structure is locked
VW's voting register is effectively a triple lock:

• Porsche SE (Piëch / Porsche families) — 31.9% of economic interest but 53.3% of voting rights in VW AG. Porsche SE is itself family-controlled.
• State of Lower Saxony — 20% stake, preserved under the Volkswagen Act of 1960 which grants the state veto rights over major strategic decisions (plant closures, takeovers, significant restructurings).
• Qatar Investment Authority — 14.6% economic interest, 17% voting rights.

Combined, these three hold >90% of voting rights. A Cevian 3% stake is a rounding error at the AGM. There is no minority-shareholder path to the supervisory board that goes through the voting register. Activism at VW has been attempted for 20+ years without a single board concession. The family, the state, and the sovereign have aligned incentives on structural continuity.
Reason 2 — China exposure is structural
VW is the largest single-country Western auto OEM in China. Three joint ventures — SAIC-Volkswagen, FAW-Volkswagen, Volkswagen Anhui — historically delivered ~40% of group profit. That profit base has collapsed over 2023-26 as BYD, Geely, Xiaomi, Li Auto, Xpeng, and the broader domestic EV ecosystem have taken share on a massive scale.

Porsche AG deliveries in China fell 28% in FY2025. VW Q1 2026 deliveries -4% globally, with China the largest contributor.

This is exactly the "Chinese commoditisation" risk Nikola called out as a Cevian-excluded category — and arguably the most important of his explicit filters. Even the CEO's own 2026 strategy (the Xpeng software partnership announced April 2026) is a defensive move, not an offensive one. Cevian does not do defensive.
Reason 3 — Auto OEMs don't earn returns
Auto OEMs are, as a category, structurally low-return businesses. VW spends >€18bn/year on R&D and >€15bn/year on capex. Capex >> D&A for most of the last decade. Reported earnings systematically overstate true owner earnings; the only return that ultimately reaches shareholders is the dividend.

Currently 10%+ dividend yield — which sounds attractive — but dividends have been cut or held in most stress periods since 2015 (diesel scandal, COVID, 2024-25 China reset). Cevian's portfolio has no auto OEM in it (Autoliv is a safety-component supplier — auto-adjacent, much higher return, not an OEM).

Even if Cevian could get a board seat (which it can't) and force an optimal portfolio restructuring (which it can't), the residual cash-generating core would still be a mass-market auto OEM fighting Chinese competition on home turf. The return on capital problem does not go away with a governance fix.

"Porsche AG is the crown jewel — just own VW as a levered way to own Porsche AG." You get ~€28bn of Porsche exposure for a €53bn VW market cap — effectively half-price Porsche. France runs structural deficits of 5%+ of GDP, debt/GDP at 112%+ and rising, and no equivalent debt-brake-exemption mechanism. France's defence industry (Thales, Dassault, Safran, Naval Group) receives DGA procurement support but the scale of the cheque is maybe half Germany's, and it's spread across a more crowded domestic supplier base.

The EU-level defence spending framework (ReArm Europe / SAFE) is a €150bn loan facility — useful, but small relative to Germany's unilateral spend, and not capable of cross-subsidising French employment in the way Germany's own budget will cross-subsidise German employment. Your "Germans will pay to keep Germans employed" instinct is precisely the political economy at work.

The signal in the index

MSCI EMU Aerospace & Defence Index +78% YTD through October 2025. That's the market already capitalising the obvious primes. The question for Cevian isn't whether to buy Rheinmetall at today's price — it's whether there is an activist-shaped industrial name where the defence kicker is still underpriced because the market is looking at the parent as a steel/materials/auto-supply conglomerate rather than as the holder of a newly-listed maritime-defence franchise.

§ 6.3 — Why VW Isn't the Expression

Real Defence Exposure, Wrong Scale

The Exposure Is Real...
VW has meaningful military-adjacent capacity via Traton (87.5% owned):

• Rheinmetall MAN Military Vehicles (RMMV) — JV between Rheinmetall and MAN Truck & Bus. Makes the HX-series 6×6/8×8 logistics trucks, the German army's standard tactical lorry. Orderbook has expanded materially post-Zeitenwende.
• Scania — military truck contracts, smaller than MAN but growing.
• VW Commercial Vehicles (Hanover) — tactical vehicle work.
• PowerCo (battery venture) — potential for military EV drivetrain and drone-grid power contracts.

The direction is correct; German defence procurement favours German-owned manufacturing capacity. Citi's March 2026 "defence pivot" note to VW was directionally not wrong.
...But the Scale Is Wrong
VW Group revenue is €326bn. Estimated direct military-vehicle revenue across RMMV + Scania defence + VWCV tactical is probably <€1bn. Even tripling that over five years, it's rounding-error at group level.

The "shed the EMs, keep the German core" reframing is structurally unworkable: the Chinese JV interest (SAIC-VW, FAW-VW) cannot be cleanly divested — JV partners are state-owned, have right of first refusal at book value, and no buyer would pay market for a losing-share franchise. A €25-30bn write-down would be the price of exit. Meanwhile "German-core VW" is the lowest-margin part of the group — the profitable brands sit in Czech Republic (Škoda), Italy (Lamborghini, Ducati), UK (Bentley), and non-German parts of Audi/Porsche supply chains. Stripping to "pure German" makes the company worse, not better.

Conclusion: VW benefits marginally from the German subsidy wave. It does not become a defence-themed investment. The governance, China, and auto-OEM-returns reasons for passing are unchanged.

§ 6.7 — Key Risks
Risk — TKMS multiple compression
If defence stocks de-rate (peace scenario, Trump-era procurement cuts), TKMS could give back its IPO pop. TKAG's 51% stake scales linearly with TKMS multiple. Mitigant: TKMS backlog is multi-year; signed contracts carry forward earnings even in a thematic pullback. The downside is probably -20-25% at TKMS, translating to -10-15% at TKAG.
Risk — Steel Europe deal collapses
Jindal offer may not close; German state may insist on local ownership; European steel market remains difficult. Mitigant: Steel Europe is valued at close to zero in today's price anyway; downside is muted. Upside comes from any monetisation event, which crystallises the stub.
Risk — Pension deficit
TKAG carries a historically large German pension liability which swings with Bund yields. Mitigant: Already a known, provisioned liability. Rising rates 2022-25 have actually shrunk the present-value deficit — the tailwind that's been flowing for three years continues in Bund yields at 3%+.

§ 7.4 — Portfolio Fit

Ahold Delhaize, DHL Group and Bayer each represent a different degree of the same activist pattern: a crown-jewel business held down by a structural encumbrance. Methodological Notes

EBITDA segmentation. Bayer does not report EBITDA by division in interim reports; allocations derived by applying the company's own 2025 margin guidance range (Crop 18-20%, Pharma 24-26%, CH 23-24%) to estimated divisional revenue. Ahold Delhaize reports segment operating income but not EBITDA; EBITDA estimated by applying a ~1.6× EBITDA/EBIT bridge consistent with grocery peer averages. DHL reports EBIT by division; EBITDA estimated using divisional D&A intensity (Express and Supply Chain higher; Forwarding lower).

Multiple ranges. Bottom of applied range = conservative case (e.g., Bayer Pharma 8× = Sanofi level). Top of range = one standard deviation above the comp set mean to reflect operational quality where relevant (e.g., Food Lion compounding streak warrants 10× rather than 8×).
