What the lens reveals

Three distinct profiles in one shortlist. Smiths and IMI are the high-ROIC compounders where the activist case is "amplify what's already working" — Cevian's role is to discipline capital allocation as cash builds. DHL and Ahold are the moderate-quality businesses where the activist case is the structural unlock — the break-up itself is what improves returns. Bayer and VW sit at the painful end — capital-destructive at the consolidated level, and where Bayer's break-up is the cure, VW has no comparable cure available because its triple-locked governance prevents one. Cevian Downside Test

Max-Loss Sizing (20% Downside Rule)

Cevian's published risk framework requires that even in the worst reasonable scenario, a position should not lose more than ~20%. Applied to each idea:
Ahold — €33 floor
Would require US gross margin to collapse + European pressure. Even so, €1.5-2bn FCF, €1bn/yr buyback, multiple floor 6× EBITDA. Grocery sector drawdowns in GFC and COVID were single-digit. Structural protection is category, not execution.
DHL — €40 floor
Trade recession + Trump tariff escalation + Verdi wage settlement above plan. Express duopoly with UPS holds transatlantic margin; Fit for Growth €1bn cost programme releases 2027. Letter mail runoff is priced and regulated.
Bayer — €33 floor
Requires Roundup Supreme Court loss + 2027 pharma disappointment + no break-up. Even then, Pharma (€4.5bn EBITDA × 8×) + Consumer Health (€1.4bn × 12×) covers net debt; Crop Science becomes free option.
Volkswagen — €75 floor
This is where VW fails the test. Porsche AG already at €43 (close to all-time low); China share loss has permanent and cumulative features; EV capex does not ease. A -30% scenario is not extraordinary — it's a mid-case if two of three known headwinds compound. 20% downside discipline cannot be maintained.
Style Synthesis

How These Ideas Fit Cevian's Playbook

The style I'm trying to show

The four names blend four influences: (i) my own preference for SOTP-driven stories with verifiable segment-level EBITDA (same analytical spine as the Amadeus pitch); (ii) Cevian's "neglected or misunderstood in out-of-favour industries" filter (Förberg / Hedge Fund Journal, 2007); (iii) Andrew's emphasis on the governance mechanism — activism only works when there is a plausible board-level intervention, not a press release; and (iv) my dad's long-held view that the best European activist opportunities come from German industrial and Dutch holding-company structures, where the conglomerate discount is both durable and, when it finally closes, violent.

The first three ideas share a structural shape: (a) a crown-jewel business that would trade at 10-15× on its own, (b) one or more encumbering legacy / declining / regulated businesses dragging the multiple down, (c) an incumbent CEO publicly resistant to breakup, (d) a credible catalyst window in 24-36 months. This is the ABB (2012-18) playbook with Power Grids; the ThyssenKrupp (2013-) playbook with the elevator spin; the Pearson playbook with the Penguin Random House exit. Cevian has run this exact pattern seven times in the last fifteen years.

Volkswagen is included deliberately as a counter-example. On pure SOTP math it looks like the single best idea on this page. In practice, its three-way ownership lock (Porsche SE + Lower Saxony + Qatar) means Cevian cannot buy a board seat even with a €3bn stake — the voting control simply does not move. The discipline of rejecting a visibly-cheap name because the unlock mechanism does not work is, in my view, the single most important thing to get right in a Cevian interview.

Why the unlock is bigger than the SOTP alone

The SOTP number is conservative on purpose. The real unlock — the thing that makes this a 5:1 Cevian idea rather than a 2:1 one — is capital-return acceleration post-separation. A US-listed AD US would do two things simultaneously: (a) access US institutional capital directly, closing the passive-ownership discount; (b) free up the parent balance sheet to return another €2-3bn/year via buyback at a higher multiple, compounding much faster than the current €1bn programme. The combined effect over a 3-year hold is 35-50% total return versus a 15-25% total return if nothing changes. This is the classic Cevian compounding pattern: fair-valued downside, 5:1 upside through a structural change that is the incumbent board's to own.

§ 2.5 — The Activist Unlock

What Cevian Can Actually Do

The Ask
Primary: dual-listing of the US business on NYSE (parent retains 70-80%) to close the passive-ownership discount. Secondary: carve-out IPO of bol.com (parent retains 80%) to recognise its standalone digital-commerce valuation. Tertiary: operating-margin catch-up to Kroger/peer levels in the US through continued price investment + Prism monetisation.

The ask is not break-up — Ahold Delhaize has better cross-regional learning than most retail conglomerates (Save for Our Customers programme delivered €1.35bn of savings in 2025). It is listing optimisation. This is a lower bar than a split and the board can approve it without operational disruption.
The Governance
Ahold Delhaize is a Dutch NV with a two-tier board (Management Board + Supervisory Board). Frans Muller (CEO since 2018) is widely expected to retire in the next 2-3 years — creating a natural transition window for strategic review. There is no incumbent activist on the register. Cevian's 2025 arrival at Akzo Nobel (also Dutch) demonstrates the firm is now operating comfortably in the Dutch governance environment.

Entry point: a 2-3% stake (~€700m-1bn, well within Cevian's typical check size) is enough to secure supervisory board consultation. 5% is the threshold at which Dutch disclosure kicks in — Cevian's preferred size.
§ 2.6 — Key Risks & Mitigants
Risk — US price war accelerates
Walmart, Costco, and Aldi are all pushing price aggressively. Aldi announced a large US expansion for 2026. Mitigant: Food Lion's 53-quarter comp streak includes the 2023-25 period of maximum inflation-driven price competition. Ahold's US banners are regional leaders, not national scale-players — they compete on local assortment and service, not on the national price spear tip. The price-war risk is structurally lower for Food Lion than for Kroger or Albertsons.
Risk — IRA pharmacy drag
The 2026 guidance calls out a ~$350m negative impact on US reported sales from the Inflation Reduction Act. Mitigant: Ahold has explicitly stated there is no impact to underlying operating income — pharmacy is low-margin revenue and the hit is a reclassification, not a cash-flow loss. The market needs a quarter or two to digest this distinction; Cevian can use that volatility to add.
Risk — Board resistance to US listing
Dutch NV boards have historically resisted dilution of home-market control. Mitigant: a dual listing is not a relisting — home market remains Amsterdam; parent retains control. Multiple precedents exist: Shell's NL/UK dual structure (collapsed 2022), Unilever's historical dual structure. The ask is listing access, not listing relocation.
Risk — Serbia / CSE politicisation
Q4 2025 European margin was hit by the Serbian government's price-cap intervention on grocery. Mitigant: CSE is ~5% of group revenue, not margin-critical. The 2023 Profi acquisition is still integrating and margin should normalise. This is the smallest risk on the page.
§ 2.7 — Why Cevian Specifically
Ahold Delhaize is the cleanest of the three ideas on the page from an activist process standpoint: no incumbent activist, no political complication, no litigation overhang, no CEO publicly entrenched in a position. What it needs is a counterparty with (a) Dutch governance fluency — which Cevian now has via Akzo Nobel; (b) US capital-markets credibility — which Cevian has via its co-investment history with Carl Icahn, its US-domiciled LPs, and its history at Autoliv (dual Swedish/US listing); (c) patience — the US dual-listing is a 2-3 year preparation, not a quarter. Cevian's 5-year horizon is exactly right.

Precedent reference: Cevian's work at SKF (Swedish industrial, dual-listed engagement) and ABB (Swiss industrial, cross-border carve-out of Power Grids to Hitachi) are the closest process templates. Both delivered 50%+ total return over the relevant hold period. The Ahold US unlock is a simpler version of that same playbook.

Engineered components, flexible hosing, rigid tubing for heating and moving fluids/gases. Two main end markets: aerospace (engine fuel and hydraulic systems for Boeing/Airbus/RTX, growing on rate ramp) and US residential HVAC (currently soft on housing market weakness, but priced in).

The conglomerate logic — or lack of it

Express earns 11-12% EBIT margins on global trade flows; PPG earns 6% on regulated domestic mail. Keeping them together subsidises one with the credibility of the other, but the market prices the combined entity at the weaker multiple. This is exactly the structural pattern Cevian broke at ABB (Power Grids carve-out) and at ThyssenKrupp (elevator spin-off).

§ 5.3 — Why Express Deserves the FedEx/UPS Multiple

The Express Franchise

Structural Position
Three integrators globally. That's it. FedEx, UPS, DHL Express. No fourth. Amazon has logistics capacity but does not offer time-definite international express at scale. Time-definite international (TDI) is the specific product category where customers cannot substitute — if your cargo is (a) medical/diagnostic samples, (b) regulated-pharmaceutical cold chain, (c) automotive or semiconductor parts where production-line downtime cost exceeds shipping cost, you use one of these three. Routing choice is frequently set at a customer's global procurement level and is sticky.

DHL's geographic bias is Europe-Asia and intra-Europe, where UPS is thinner. On those lanes DHL Express is effectively a duopoly with FedEx. The 11.8% EBIT margin in Q3 2025 — with trade volumes falling — demonstrates the pricing power.
Why It's Not Priced That Way
Four reasons the group multiple compresses the Express multiple:

(1) Post & Parcel Germany's secular letter-mail decline drags the blended growth profile. (2) State-linked ownership — KfW holds ~16% and the German government has implicit influence over strategic decisions via the regulated postal business. (3) Verdi labour relations in Germany — wage settlements are a recurring overhang. (4) Global Forwarding volatility — this segment's earnings swing with freight rates and creates noise around cleaner Express numbers.

A standalone DHL Express would eliminate all four overhangs in one step. FedEx's $85bn EV at 10-11× EBITDA on broadly similar EBITDA ($8bn) is the reference point. UPS is similar. Standalone DHL Express at 10-11× → €45-50bn EV, captured inside a group EV of €79bn today.

§ 5.5 — The Activist Unlock

The Ask
Primary: legal separation of Post & Parcel Germany into a standalone, German-listed regulated postal utility (parent retains 50-75% initially, reduces over time). Immediate effect: group re-prices on the remaining Express / Supply Chain / Global Forwarding / eCommerce mix — a cleaner, higher-margin, internationally-comparable logistics franchise that trades at a blended 9-10× rather than a blended 7× today.

This is the ABB Power Grids carve-out template, run in the opposite direction: at ABB, Cevian pushed to sell a legacy-heavy unit (Power Grids to Hitachi) while keeping the growth core. The Governance Complexity
This is the hardest of the three names to execute on. Three complicating factors:

(1) KfW (German state development bank) holds ~16% — per Public Technologies April 2026 reporting, BlackRock raised its voting-rights stake to 6.6% around the same time, making KfW and BlackRock the two largest shareholders. KfW is a political actor as much as a financial one.

(2) Verdi (the German services union) is organised across Post & Parcel Germany and has mobilised against restructuring before. The 2025 announcement of 8,000 headcount reductions was accompanied by significant labour protest. Any separation triggers labour co-determination process under German Mitbestimmung.

(3) Regulated-postal licence obligations — under German postal law, the "universal service" obligation (daily mail delivery to every German household) cannot simply be shed. A PPG separation must carry the regulatory framework with it, which is a multi-year legislative process.

None of this is fatal — Cevian's precedent at ThyssenKrupp (IG Metall, state-linked shareholders, German co-determination) is directly analogous. But it makes this a 3-5 year engagement, not a 2-year one.

§ 6.5 — The Activist Unlock

What Cevian Can Actually Do

The Mechanism
Bayer is a Supervisory Board (Aufsichtsrat) company under German co-determination. Half the board is employee representatives; half is shareholder representatives. Cevian's historical playbook in Germany (ThyssenKrupp, Bilfinger) has been to engage on the shareholder side of the supervisory board — putting people around the table in nomination and strategy committees, not in the press.

The specific ask is not an immediate full three-way break-up. Why Consumer Health first
Four reasons. (1) Cleanest separation — no shared IP with Pharma, no shared manufacturing with Crop. (2) Clearest comp — Haleon / Kenvue / Opella set the multiple. (3) Anderson's stated objection (2024 earnings call: Consumer Health is "reliably generating cash flow") is exactly why it is most valuable to someone else — a pure-play CHC operator would pay up for predictable category cash flow. Bluebell's 2023 letter and Inclusive Capital's subsequent position make this the publicly-understood activist ask. Cevian entering would not be a surprise attack — it would be a collaborative acceleration of a strategic review that is already in the board's inbox.

§ 7.3 — Why the Discount Persists

Three Structural Reasons, Each of Which Fails the Cevian Filter

Reason 1 — Ownership structure is locked
VW's voting register is effectively a triple lock:

• Porsche SE (Piëch / Porsche families) — 31.9% of economic interest but 53.3% of voting rights in VW AG. Porsche SE is itself family-controlled.
• State of Lower Saxony — 20% stake, preserved under the Volkswagen Act of 1960 which grants the state veto rights over major strategic decisions (plant closures, takeovers, significant restructurings).
• Qatar Investment Authority — 14.6% economic interest, 17% voting rights.

Combined, these three hold >90% of voting rights. A Cevian 3% stake is a rounding error at the AGM. There is no minority-shareholder path to the supervisory board that goes through the voting register. Activism at VW has been attempted for 20+ years without a single board concession. The family, the state, and the sovereign have aligned incentives on structural continuity.
Reason 2 — China exposure is structural
VW is the largest single-country Western auto OEM in China. Three joint ventures — SAIC-Volkswagen, FAW-Volkswagen, Volkswagen Anhui — historically delivered ~40% of group profit. That profit base has collapsed over 2023-26 as BYD, Geely, Xiaomi, Li Auto, Xpeng, and the broader domestic EV ecosystem have taken share on a massive scale.

Porsche AG deliveries in China fell 28% in FY2025. VW Q1 2026 deliveries -4% globally, with China the largest contributor.

This is exactly the "Chinese commoditisation" risk Nikola called out as a Cevian-excluded category — and arguably the most important of his explicit filters. Even the CEO's own 2026 strategy (the Xpeng software partnership announced April 2026) is a defensive move, not an offensive one. Cevian does not do defensive.
Reason 3 — Auto OEMs don't earn returns
Auto OEMs are, as a category, structurally low-return businesses. VW spends >€18bn/year on R&D and >€15bn/year on capex. Capex >> D&A for most of the last decade. Reported earnings systematically overstate true owner earnings; the only return that ultimately reaches shareholders is the dividend.

Currently 10%+ dividend yield — which sounds attractive — but dividends have been cut or held in most stress periods since 2015 (diesel scandal, COVID, 2024-25 China reset). Cevian's portfolio has no auto OEM in it (Autoliv is a safety-component supplier — auto-adjacent, much higher return, not an OEM).

Even if Cevian could get a board seat (which it can't) and force an optimal portfolio restructuring (which it can't), the residual cash-generating core would still be a mass-market auto OEM fighting Chinese competition on home turf. The return on capital problem does not go away with a governance fix.

The "shed the EMs, keep the German core" reframing is structurally unworkable: the Chinese JV interest (SAIC-VW, FAW-VW) cannot be cleanly divested — JV partners are state-owned, have right of first refusal at book value, and no buyer would pay market for a losing-share franchise. A €25-30bn write-down would be the price of exit. Meanwhile "German-core VW" is the lowest-margin part of the group — the profitable brands sit in Czech Republic (Škoda), Italy (Lamborghini, Ducati), UK (Bentley), and non-German parts of Audi/Porsche supply chains. Stripping to "pure German" makes the company worse, not better.

Conclusion: VW benefits marginally from the German subsidy wave. It does not become a defence-themed investment. The governance, China, and auto-OEM-returns reasons for passing are unchanged.

§ 11.4 — Portfolio Fit

Four shapes, one playbook — and one counter-example

Ahold Delhaize, DHL Group and Bayer each represent a different degree of the same activist pattern: a crown-jewel business held down by a structural encumbrance. Ahold is the simplest — a listing-venue arbitrage, no break-up required, friendly board environment. DHL is the most structurally analogous to ABB — a world-class franchise (Express) trapped inside a regulated German legacy (Post & Parcel), with a five-year political process required to separate them. Bayer is the most extreme version — three separately valuable units, with the CEO publicly resistant to change and the litigation overhang providing political cover, but +94% off the lows, which compresses the entry-price case.

Volkswagen is the deliberate exclusion. The asset-stacked-at-near-zero-stub is the textbook activist-SOTP setup, but the triple voting lock (Porsche SE + Lower Saxony + Qatar), the Chinese secular headwind, and the sub-cost-of-capital auto-OEM returns profile make it exactly the kind of name Cevian should be able to explain passing on. Being able to describe why VW is cheap — and why the reasons are uncorrectable by any activist, not just Cevian — is the single most useful thing to rehearse for the Fabian coffee chat.

Methodological Notes

EBITDA segmentation. Bayer does not report EBITDA by division in interim reports; allocations derived by applying the company's own 2025 margin guidance range (Crop 18-20%, Pharma 24-26%, CH 23-24%) to estimated divisional revenue. Ahold Delhaize reports segment operating income but not EBITDA; EBITDA estimated by applying a ~1.6× EBITDA/EBIT bridge consistent with grocery peer averages. DHL reports EBIT by division; EBITDA estimated using divisional D&A intensity (Express and Supply Chain higher; Forwarding lower).

Multiple ranges. Bottom of applied range = conservative case (e.g., Bayer Pharma 8× = Sanofi level). Top of range = one standard deviation above the comp set mean to reflect operational quality where relevant (e.g., Food Lion compounding streak warrants 10× rather than 8×). SOTP midpoints used to derive headline upside figures.

Currency. All figures in EUR unless specifically marked. Non-EUR comps (Kroger, Kenvue, FedEx, UPS, Costco) quoted in USD for multiple comparability — the multiple is currency-neutral at this level of precision.
